Small kindness found in wake of book theft worth millions Many of the books in James Strand’s stunning collection are still lost. But some have found their way home.

May 11, 2025 | Oregonian, The (Portland, OR)
Author/Byline: Steve Duin | Page: 001 | Section: A
1067 Words
Read News Document

Whenever they’re in the mood for adventure, Jamie McClure and Nate Brown leave the rabbits and lush gardens at their northwest Salem home ... and crack open a storage unit they’ve corralled in a foreclosure auction.

Last December, the couple purchased the sight-unseen contents of a unit in Lyons, on the Santiam Highway, for $60.

They put on masks and gloves, then rolled back the door to discover an odd mix. Meth pipes and other drug paraphernalia. A bucket filled with 900 pocketknives. And several boxes that warranted closer inspection back home.

McClure was making dinner when her partner brought two books in from the garage. The copy of Ray Bradbury’s iconic 1953 novel, “Fahrenheit 451,” lit up the room.

McClure grew up with books on her grandparents’ farm. “I knew instantly what it was,” she says. “It was old and perfect. Even across the room, it gave me goosebumps. Beyond the cultural significance, I knew it was priceless.”

She did some research into Bradbury-inscribed first editions, then called Scott Givens at Crooked House Books in Portland. Givens was rather skeptical until McClure texted him several photos of her find.

“I’m just about to lose my mind,” Givens says. “I’m telling my family, ‘This is the best lead I’ve gotten in my entire life.’ ”

But credible book dealers are always wary about the provenance of such books, and Bradbury’s inscription — “Happy Birthday, Donn! + Love!” — set off a small alarm.

Givens reached out to Scott Brown, another Portland antiquarian book dealer. Brown has worked tirelessly for the last 18 months to document and recover the books and comics stolen from James Strand’s dark and lonely home in Lents in the summer of 2023.

“Does Donn have two N’s?” Brown asked Givens. “Then, it belonged to James Strand.”

As I wrote 20 months ago, the reclusive Strand – who worked in The Oregonian print shop for 54 years – died alone in that Lents’ house.

Drug dealers and street-gang members waltzed in long before the medical examiner. Before and after Strand’s body was removed, they ransacked his library, stealing first editions, manuscripts and original art.

The value of those treasures? The brazenness of the theft and callous indifference of Portland police? The tragedy that much of Strand’s collection may be forever lost?

In my 2023 column, I probably undersold all of that.

The Bradbury book is worth as much as $15,000. The second “book” that Nate Brown brought in from his Salem garage? The original manuscript of H.P. Lovecraft’s 1924 short story, “Under the Pyramids,” which Lovecraft wrote on the back of old business letters.

Strand bought those pages years ago from Lloyd Currey, an antiquarian book dealer in New York, for $55,000. When I asked Curry if the Lovecraft manuscript was a unique or prescient purchase, Currey scoffed.

“He bought millions of dollars of material from me,” Currey said. “Strand’s collection is probably the finest ever put together of science fiction and fantasy dealing with Lovecraft, (publisher) Arkham House and the Arkham House writers. I can’t even think of an institution with a better collection.”

While Portland cops showed no interest in what Givens calls “the greatest book theft in the history of book thefts,” the FBI eventually made two arrests: Joshua Blaine Munsey – who had 11 previous felony convictions on his sheet – and Doran Billie Hardman, who has an extensive drug history.

Both pleaded guilty to felony possession of firearms, and are now in imprisoned at the federal prison in Sheridan. Munsey was sentenced to 75 months, Hardman to 30.

“Firearms have no bearing on the James Strand case,” an FBI spokesman noted. When the FBI caught the thieves, they were packing semi-automatic handguns, and federal gun charges carry far more weight than possession of stolen property.

But let’s return to Jamie McClure and Nate Brown. After learning of their storage-locker find, an FBI agent called McClure. When he introduced himself, McClure burst out laughing.

“I couldn’t help it. The FBI? I hung up,” she says. “It was so absurd. When he called back, I hung up again.”

Then Susan Hasty, James Strand’s niece, called the Salem couple. She explained what she knew about her uncle and the brutal theft of his collection.

“I went two weeks thinking we had an actual fortune,” McClure says. “But when I talked to Susie, it was very clear that it wasn’t our family fortune. It was someone else’s. Of course we went through a period of mourning. Shock. Grief. Then I was just glad we found it.”

Jamie and Nate invited Hasty to their Salem home. And that copy of “Fahrenheit 451?”

“They brought this book out and handed it over like it was a small baby,” Hasty says. “It was really sweet. I felt like this was the way it should have been: Jim’s book was being cared for like it was something really special.”

Too many of Strand’s other books are still lost in the wild. While the FBI has not made any arrests in the last 12 months, a spokesperson said pending and uncharged cases remain, and that “individuals associated with the BROOD and European Kindred ‘EK’ street/prison gangs were involved in the theft.”

The FBI estimates $1 million in stolen books and comics have been recovered, but no one knows how many Strand books have disappeared into personal collections, burn piles or other storage lockers. If Strand kept a meticulous inventory, it disappeared in the looting of his home.

“He had a fantastic Stephen King collection, and we have almost no record of it,” says Brown, the book dealer. “The few things that were recovered were extraordinary. That makes me think a hundred extraordinary books are gone, and we don’t know what they were.”

And that’s why Scott Givens at Crooked House Books will not forget the phone call from Jamie McClure.

“This was a crime against our literary culture,” Givens says. “Strand didn’t collect the canonical authors, Steinbeck or Hemingway, just the pulp fiction he loved, but those books are extremely scarce, and in some ways even more important.”

McClure only sent Givens photos of the Bradbury book; she might have remained quiet about the Lovecraft manuscript, which is now worth at least $100,000.

“But she does the right thing,” Givens says. “She went through her stuff and found the single most valuable book, and handed it over. She was excited to own the books for a few minutes, but being part of this larger story was worth more than putting the book on eBay.

“I think that’s a beautiful story.”
